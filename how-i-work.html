How I Work

Version 10.0

My Role

I build businesses on products customers love — and I try to define the category before anyone else does.


I own the full stack: technical architecture, product direction, and the organization that executes it. I do my best work in lean, fast-moving environments where the goal is to be first to market. Optimizing what already exists is someone else’s job.


I’ve personally written production code, architected platforms, and built products from scratch with 5-person teams that grew to $30M in revenue. When I say “show me the code,” I mean it.


If you report to me, I see that as a partnership. The best work comes from a few deep, trusted working relationships. That’s where I invest.

About Me

I grew up in two places: Chicago’s South Side and rural Central Florida near the Bible Belt. Both working class, both far from what’s considered typical in white-collar tech.


I’m the first high school graduate in my family on either side. My roots are tied to Chicago’s Mexican working-class community — we’ve been in the same neighborhood for over a century. I’ve been self-supporting since I was 17.


My academic path wasn’t a straight line: Fine Arts at the School of the Art Institute of Chicago, Latin American Studies at the University of Illinois Chicago, Social Sciences at the University of Chicago, Computer Science at DePaul — all while working full time. The throughline is systems thinking. I see organizations, products, and teams as complex systems. I look for outlier perspectives and challenge default assumptions.


20 years in Chicago — a decade building platforms as an IC, then a decade leading the organizations around them.


The past five years at AWS and Meta were deliberate: I wanted to understand how planetary-scale systems actually work — S3, EKS, and now Meta’s ad-serving infrastructure supporting billions in annual revenue. I’m ready to bring that back to company building at the frontier — scaling platforms in markets where the infrastructure gap is the opportunity, not the obstacle.

How I Lead

I’m a transformation specialist. Same pattern at every company: take over an org that needs a step change, restructure around Conway’s Law, introduce modern practices, double output, cut costs. I’m not a caretaker.


I move fast. Doubled engineering headcount without a recruiter. Stood up modern DevOps practices in my first week. Reset quality assurance (QA) in two months. Grew a team from 1 to 9 engineers in one quarter. Speed isn’t recklessness — it’s respect for time.


I lead across engineering and product. I help teams connect their work to customers, stay focused on outcomes, and ship value every day.


I build to think. A working prototype beats a doc, a slide deck, or a meeting. If you want to make a point, build something.

Working Together

Values

Delivery – Ship on time, no major defects, no sloppy architectures, drama-free deployments. We’ll miss sometimes. When we do, we get back up, learn, and deliver better next time.

Ownership – See a need, fill a need. Step in, help out, do what needs doing — regardless of whether it falls neatly in your job description. This works whether you’re a chief executive or a grocery store bagger.

Process, not dogma – Process serves the team, not the other way around. We keep it fluid and pragmatic. We iterate on process the same way we iterate on code.

Psychological safety – Teams do their best work when they feel safe to speak up, stumble, and grow. This is not a perk. It’s a prerequisite.

Diversity and inclusion – Diverse teams bring broader perspectives and better thinking. But beyond outcomes, this is about justice and equity. I resist homogeneity.

What I Measure

Customer delight – Do our customers enjoy using what we built?

On-time delivery – Are we hitting key milestones with quality?

Operational health – Is what we build stable, secure, and supportable in production?

Escaped defects – How often do critical issues reach production?

Team velocity over time – Are we improving, or just busy?

How I Communicate

Direct. I get to clarity fast. It’s a form of respect.

Collaborative. I have a lot of ideas and I like to talk them through. The best ideas come from teams, not individuals.

Clear on deadlines. If I need something by a specific date or time, I’ll say so. No guesswork.

What I Need From You

Be direct. Say what’s on your mind. Honesty over harmony.

Be technical. Show me the code. Walk me through the design. Pairing is the fastest way I get it.

Call out my misses. If I did something that made your job harder, tell me. It was probably a mistake, and I want to fix it.

Be a creative partner. Got a wild idea or a small spark? Bring it. Let’s build it.

Be specific when asking for feedback. The more targeted the ask, the more useful I can be.

First Principles

On Technology

Build to think. A working prototype says more than a doc, a slide, or a meeting ever could. Action beats talk.

Innovation is emergent. Real innovation rarely announces itself. It shows up in rough form, at the edges. Messy, unpredictable, and powerful — that’s what makes it real. (Kuhn and Feyerabend would agree.)

Let go to move forward. The cost of clinging to old, comfortable ideas is irrelevance. Stay open, stay curious, throw out what no longer serves.

On Systems

Great systems share common traits – extensible, modular, polymorphic, instrumented. These keep systems resilient and ready for change.

Trust nothing by default. Of the eight fallacies of distributed computing, the most dangerous is assuming the network is reliable. It isn’t. Design like failure is inevitable — because it is.

DevOps is a mindset, not a title. Teams that build, run, and support their own systems move faster and break less. Owning your code in production is the ultimate feedback loop.

On Organizations

Conway’s Law is real. The systems you build mirror the structures you work within. If you want clean architecture, design a clean org.

The Peter Principle is real. People get promoted beyond their capabilities not from lack of potential, but from lack of support and role clarity. I work to prevent this.

The player/coach model: use with caution. It can work — but only in small teams, with senior people, and clear role separation. Too often it’s a way to over-promote strong individual contributors into management without training or space to succeed.
